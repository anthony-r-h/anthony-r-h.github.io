# Introduction

Agentic AI is not a separate primitive asset class from capital. It is a service flow of delegated cognitive execution generated by a composite stock consisting of compute infrastructure, model capital, data assets, and organizational complements, together with scarce human supervision and verification.

- everyone becomes a builder, which is this vibe code revolution call to action
- the 10x engineer
- code is cheap, software is expensive

# Literature Review

[Acemoglu-Autor-Johnson-2026](/files/Builders/References/Acemoglu-Autor-Johnson-2026.pdf)

- The current economics literature mostly models AI as task-performing capital, an automation/augmentation technology, or a coordination technology.
- The dominant framework is the Acemoglu/Autor/Restrepo task model. Automation raises productivity but also displaces labor from tasks it used to perform.
- Whether wages rise or fall depends on the balance between the positive productivity effect and the negative displacement effect.
- AI is treated as capital or an automation technology that changes task assignment.

[Brynjolfsson-Li-Raymond-2023](/files/Builders/References/Brynjolfsson-Li-Raymond-2023.pdf)

- Generative AI tools acts as compressors: productivity boosts for low-skill workers, and little effect on high-skill workers.
- Non-autonomous / assistive AI often helps lower performers more and compresses within-occupation inequality.

[Shahidi-Rusak-Manning-2025](/files/Builders/References/Shahidi-Rusak-Manning-2025.pdf)

- AI agents can search, negotiate, contract, transact, coordinate, and act on behalf of principals. That pushes the analysis from production theory into transaction costs, contracting, market design, organization, and principal-agent problems.
- AI agents may dramatically lower search, communication, contracting, identity-verification, and bargaining costs. That means the relevant economic effect is not only "replace labor in a task," but "change which market forms and organizational forms are feasible."

[Chatterji-Rock-Talamas-2025](/files/Builders/References/Chatterji-Rock-Talamas-2025.pdf)

- Autonomous / agentic AI can favor top talent, compress hierarchy, reduce manager demand, and raise wage dispersion in some regimes.

# Agentic Capacity In Production Function

- How does the move from assistive AI to autonomous agentic AI change equilibrium wages and employment across heterogeneous workers when tasks differ in verifiability and workers differ in knowledge?
- "AI as capital" is the stock view. "Agentic labor" is the service flow produced by that stock once you combine models, compute, data, and oversight into actual delegated task completion.

$$
Y_t = F\big(K_t, H_t, Z_t\big)
$$

where:

* $K_t$: ordinary capital
* $H_t$: human labor / human capital
* $Z_t$: **agentic services**

Think of $Z_t$ as coming from underlying stocks:

$$
Z_t = \Phi\big(C_t, M_t, D_t, O_t, S_t\big)
$$

where:

* $C_t$ = compute capital
* $M_t$ = model capital
* $D_t$ = data / knowledge assets
* $O_t$ = organizational capital and integration
* $S_t$ = supervision / verification capacity of humans

This avoids the category error of saying "AI is not capital," while still giving you a distinct object that behaves like delegated labor.

## Why is agentic services different than capital services?

- Ordinary capital can not make decisions
- AI agents can plan, call tools, interact with other agents, and adapt to meet an objective
- Non-rival software layer
    - The model weights and code are largely non-rival once created, while inference compute is rival.
    - Underlying stocks behind agentic capacity is part software, part infrastructure

## Modelling

Agentic capacity as a composite of several accumulating stocks.

$$
A_t = \Omega(C_t, M_t, D_t, O_t, S_t)
$$

Is the effective installed ability to run delegated cognitive work.

Then agentic services (used in production function)

$$
Z_t = u_t \cdot A_t
$$

where $u_t \in [0,1]$ is utilization.

So the relationship is:

$$
(C_t, M_t, D_t, O_t, S_t) \rightarrow A_t \rightarrow Z_t \rightarrow Y_t
$$

Conceptual reduced-form representation

$$
A_t = g_t \times n_t \times q_t
$$

where:

* $g_t$: Frontier capability, what the best available model can do
* $n_t$: Deployed concurrent capacity, how many effective agent-hours you can run
* $q_t$: Reliability/autonomy, how much of that can be trusted with limited supervision

Then:

* **frontier capability** grows through model R&D, training compute, data, and algorithmic progress
* **deployed capacity** grows through data center / GPU / inference investment
* **reliability** grows through evaluation, tooling, orchestration, domain adaptation, and organizational learning

# Sectoral Labor

Raw agentic capacity does not directly become GDP, it must be converted into useful project execution by humans and firms.

So for software project $j$, define shipped output:

$$
y_j = G(c_j, s_j, v_j, o_j, m_j)
$$

where:

* $c_j$: code generation
* $s_j$: specification
* $v_j$: verification / testing
* $o_j$: integration / coordination / deployment
* $m_j$: maintenance / iteration

Now connect this to agentic services.

Let project $j$ get some allocation $z_j$ of aggregate agentic services, with:

$$
\sum_j z_j \le Z_t
$$

Then code generation is:

$$
c_j = h^R_j + \alpha_j z_j
$$

where:

* $h^R_j$: human routine execution time
* $z_j$: agentic execution assigned to the project
* $\alpha_j$: effectiveness of agentic services on this project

This is the first connection.

**Agentic capacity enters the software sector by making code production cheap.**

But software output is not just code.

So the rest of the project remains:

$$
x_j = X(s_j, v_j, o_j, m_j)
$$

and output is better thought of as something like:

$$
y_j = \min \left\{ \frac{c_j}{\kappa}, x_j \right\}
$$

This is the formal version of:

**code is cheap, software is expensive**

You can flood the project with code, but shipped value is bottlenecked by specification, testing, integration, and maintenance.

That is the direct connection to the earlier model.

## The Builder

Split agentic services by worker-level conversion. That is how the worker heterogeneity enters the aggregate model.

Let worker $i$ have builder skill $b_i$, meaning ability to:

* define the problem well
* decompose work
* prompt / orchestrate agents
* detect bad outputs
* recover from failures
* integrate outputs into a working system

Then effective agentic services under worker $i$'s control are:

$$
\tilde z_i = \phi(b_i) \cdot z_i
$$

with $\phi'(b_i) > 0$.

So two workers may get the same raw agentic capacity $z_i$, but the better builder converts it into much more useful output.

This is the bridge to the "10x engineer" idea.

It is not:

> one person types code 10x faster

It is:

> one person can productively supervise, route, verify, and integrate much more agentic execution

So the real modern leverage term is:

$$
\text{output}_i \propto \phi(b_i)\, z_i
$$

### Everyone becomes a builder

This is the extensive-margin version.

Suppose starting a project has fixed cost $F_t$. Then entry occurs if expected project value exceeds fixed cost.

$$
\pi_j = p_j y_j - p_z z_j - w h_j - F_t
$$

Enter if $\pi_j > 0$.

Now let agentic services reduce fixed costs of creating and testing software:

$$
F_t = F_0 - \lambda Z_t
$$

or more precisely at the individual level:

$$
F_{i,t} = F_0 - \lambda \phi(b_i) z_i
$$

Then as $Z_t$ rises, and as more people learn builder skill $b_i$, more projects become viable.

That is the economic translation of:

**everyone becomes a builder**

It does **not** mean everyone becomes equally productive.
It means the **threshold to attempt building falls**.

So the model predicts:

* more software projects
* more internal tooling
* more niche products
* more small-team entry
* more experimentation

This is how higher economic output can come from reduced friction, even if some incumbent jobs are displaced.

### Distributional effects

Now split human labor into two types.

* $H^R_t$: routine implementation labor
* $H^B_t$: builder / supervisory / judgment labor

Then software project production is something like:

$$
c_j = h^R_j + \alpha \phi(b_i) z_j
$$

$$
x_j = X(h^B_j, O_t, S_t)
$$

So:

* routine labor is more directly substituted by agentic services
* builder labor is complemented by agentic services

That gives the wage logic:

$$
w^R_t \downarrow \quad \text{as } Z_t \uparrow
$$

$$
w^B_t \uparrow \quad \text{as } Z_t \uparrow \text{ if builder skill is scarce}
$$

This is the formal version of your intuition:

* low-capacity cognitive labor gets substituted
* high-capacity builder labor gets leveraged

But the key is that this is **not** automatic for all high-skill labor. It is specific to labor that converts raw agentic services into shipped value.

#### Why low-capacity human labor still exists?

Even if $g_t$ is very high, output still depends on:

* $S_t$: supervision / verification capacity
* $O_t$: organizational capital
* context-specific specification
* deployment and maintenance constraints

So high model capability does not eliminate labor unless those complements are also abundant.

## 10. The key prediction

The model predicts that agentic progress does **not** mainly reward "people who can code."

It rewards people who can:

* choose projects
* specify tasks
* orchestrate agentic execution
* verify outputs
* integrate systems
* manage deployment under constraints

So the real scarce factor is not raw coding labor.
It is **builder-conversion capacity**.

That is the missing connection between your earlier aggregate model and the builder / 10x / cheap-code story.

## 11. The next refinement

The natural next step is to make $b_i$ itself dynamic:

$$
b_{i,t+1} = (1-\delta_b)b_{i,t} + \chi \cdot \text{learning-by-doing}_i
$$

Then you can ask the big question:

> does agentic AI democratize builder skill by teaching everyone to build, or does it concentrate returns by letting already-strong builders scale faster than everyone else?

That is, I think, the core research question sitting inside your framework.
